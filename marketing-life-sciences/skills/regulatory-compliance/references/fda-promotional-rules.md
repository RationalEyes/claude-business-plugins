# FDA Promotional Advertising Rules

<!-- Life Sciences Addition -->

Reference guide for US FDA regulations governing pharmaceutical and medical device promotional materials.

## Governing Authority

The **Office of Prescription Drug Promotion (OPDP)**, part of the FDA's Center for Drug Evaluation and Research (CDER), regulates prescription drug advertising and promotional labeling. For biologics, the Center for Biologics Evaluation and Research (CBER) has parallel authority. For medical devices, the Office of Compliance within the Center for Devices and Radiological Health (CDRH) oversees promotional activities.

## Key Regulatory Framework

### Federal Food, Drug, and Cosmetic Act (FD&C Act)

- **Section 502** — defines misbranding, including labeling that is false or misleading
- **Section 502(n)** — requires advertisements for prescription drugs to include established name, quantitative formula, and brief summary of side effects, contraindications, and effectiveness

### Code of Federal Regulations (CFR)

- **21 CFR 202** — prescription drug advertising regulations
- **21 CFR 201.100** — labeling requirements
- **21 CFR 99** — dissemination of information on off-label uses (limited circumstances)

## Categories of FDA-Regulated Promotional Materials

### Product Claim Advertisements

Present the product name, at least one approved indication, and make efficacy or safety claims. Must include:
- Generic name (established name) with the same prominence as brand name
- Brief summary of prescribing information (print) or major statement of risks (broadcast)
- Fair balance of efficacy and risk information
- At least one approved use

### Reminder Advertisements

Present the product name (brand and/or generic) without any representation or suggestion about the product's efficacy or safety. Exempt from brief summary requirements. Cannot include:
- Indication or use
- Dosage or dosage form (beyond what is in the name)
- Efficacy or safety claims
- Cost comparison that implies superiority

**Restriction:** Reminder ads are not permitted for products with black box warnings.

### Help-Seeking Advertisements

Describe a disease or condition and advise the audience to consult a physician. Do not name a specific product. These are not regulated as drug advertisements by FDA but may be regulated by FTC.

## FDA Enforcement Actions

### Untitled Letters

Issued for less serious violations. Request voluntary correction. Published on the OPDP website. Common triggers:
- Inadequate fair balance
- Broadening of indication
- Misleading data presentation
- Failure to include required risk information

### Warning Letters

Issued for serious violations that pose a threat to public health. Demand immediate corrective action. May require:
- Cease distribution of the violative material
- Issue corrective communications
- Submit a corrective action plan

### Other Enforcement

- Seizure of misbranded products
- Injunctions against further distribution
- Criminal prosecution (in egregious cases)
- Consent decrees

## Specific OPDP Requirements

### Brief Summary (Print Ads)

Print advertisements that make product claims must include a brief summary relating to side effects, contraindications, and effectiveness. The brief summary typically reproduces:
- Contraindications
- Warnings and Precautions
- Adverse Reactions
- Drug Interactions (if clinically significant)

### Major Statement (Broadcast Ads)

Television and radio advertisements must include a major statement of the product's most important risks. Additionally, broadcast ads must make "adequate provision" for consumers to access the full prescribing information, typically through:
- A toll-free phone number
- A website URL
- Reference to a print ad (magazines, newspapers)
- Advice to ask their doctor or pharmacist

### Fair Balance Standard

The FDA interprets fair balance as requiring:
- Risk information presented with a depth and detail comparable to benefit information
- No minimization of risks through layout, design, or typography
- No overemphasis of efficacy through selective data presentation
- Risk information in the audio portion of broadcast ads (not just visual text)

### Substantiation Requirements

- All claims must be supported by substantial evidence from adequate and well-controlled clinical investigations
- Data presentations must be consistent with the approved labeling
- Comparative claims require adequate direct comparative data
- Statistical claims must include appropriate context (confidence intervals, p-values)

## Digital and Social Media Guidance

### FDA Guidance Documents for Digital Promotion

- **Internet/Social Media Platforms with Character Space Limitations (2014)** — one-click rule for risk information in space-limited platforms
- **Internet/Social Media Platforms: Correcting Misinformation (2014)** — requirements when correcting third-party misinformation
- **Presenting Risk Information in Prescription Drug and Medical Device Promotion (2009)** — risk presentation principles applicable to digital

### Key Digital Rules

- Sponsored links must include risk information or a direct link to risk information
- Social media posts making product claims require fair balance (or link to it) within the same post
- User-generated content on company-controlled channels may need moderation for off-label claims
- Paid search ads with product claims need adequate risk communication
- Influencer/KOL social media posts about products must comply with the same standards as company-generated content

## Pre-Submission and Post-Marketing Requirements

### Form FDA 2253

All promotional materials and labeling for prescription drugs must be submitted to OPDP at the time of initial dissemination or publication using Form FDA 2253. This includes:
- Print advertisements
- Television and radio advertisements
- Promotional labeling (detail aids, brochures, booklets)
- Digital promotional materials (websites, email campaigns, social media)

### Pre-Launch Review

- Promotional materials cannot be disseminated before the product is approved
- Launch materials should be submitted to OPDP at the time of first use
- Some companies voluntarily submit materials pre-approval for advisory comments (not binding)

## REMS and Communication Plans

If a product has a Risk Evaluation and Mitigation Strategy (REMS), promotional materials must be consistent with the REMS requirements. REMS may require:
- Specific elements to assure safe use (ETASU)
- Medication guides distributed to patients
- Communication plans to healthcare providers
- Restricted distribution programs
